PERSONAL INDEPENDENCE PAYMENT — MANDATORY RECONSIDERATION REQUEST

Name: Sarah Mitchell  |  DOB: 14 March 1978

NI Number: AB 12 34 56 C  |  Reference: PIP/2024/447821

Date: 17 April 2025

To Whom It May Concern,

I am writing to formally request a mandatory reconsideration of the decision made on 3 April 2025 to award me zero points under both the Daily Living and Mobility components of Personal Independence Payment. I believe this decision does not accurately reflect the full extent of my conditions or the impact they have on my daily life, and I respectfully request that it be reviewed in full.

My Conditions

I have been diagnosed with fibromyalgia and generalised anxiety disorder by my GP, Dr. Anwar Hussain. Both conditions are long-term, fluctuating in nature, and significantly affect my ability to carry out everyday tasks safely, reliably, repeatedly, and without pain — the full legal standard required under PIP assessment criteria.

I would draw the decision maker's attention to the fact that under the PIP assessment regulations, activities must be assessed against whether they can be completed safely, to an acceptable standard, repeatedly, and within a reasonable time period. I respectfully submit that I am unable to meet this standard across multiple activity descriptors.

Why I Believe the Decision is Wrong

The assessment did not reflect my condition on a typical day. Like many people with fibromyalgia, my symptoms fluctuate significantly — however the assessment appeared to be based solely on my presentation on that single day, rather than my overall functional ability across a range of days. This is not consistent with the DWP's own guidance on fluctuating conditions.

Preparing food: I am unable to prepare a cooked meal safely without assistance. Due to chronic pain in my hands and arms I cannot safely hold or manage kitchen equipment for any sustained period. On my worst days — which occur at least four times per week — I am unable to stand at a cooker at all. I would submit that this satisfies descriptor 1b at a minimum, attracting two points.

Managing medication: I require daily reminders to take my medication due to the cognitive effects of fibromyalgia, commonly known as fibro fog. Without prompting I regularly forget doses which significantly worsens my symptoms. I would submit this satisfies descriptor 7b, attracting one point.

Moving around: I am unable to walk more than 20 metres without significant pain. I use a walking aid on most days and have fallen on three occasions in the past six months. I would submit this satisfies descriptor E under the Mobility component, attracting ten points and qualifying me for the enhanced rate.

Supporting Evidence

In support of this reconsideration I am enclosing the following: a letter from my GP confirming my diagnoses and their impact; a physiotherapy report dated January 2025; a letter from my counsellor Jane Davies MBACP confirming the psychological impact and level of support required; and a personal statement from my daughter confirming the daily practical assistance she provides.

Legal Basis

Under the Social Security (Personal Independence Payment) Regulations 2013 the assessment must consider whether activities can be carried out safely, to an acceptable standard, repeatedly, and within a reasonable time. I submit the original assessment failed to apply this standard correctly. Furthermore under the Equality Act 2010 I am a disabled person entitled to have my conditions considered in full.

I respectfully ask that this decision be reconsidered in full. If this reconsideration is unsuccessful I wish it to be noted that I intend to appeal to the First-tier Tribunal.

Yours faithfully,
Sarah Mitchell

NI Number: CD 98 76 54 E  |  Reference: PIP/2024/583921

Date: 17 April 2025

To Whom It May Concern,

I am writing to formally request a mandatory reconsideration of the decision made on 28 March 2025 to award me zero points under both the Daily Living and Mobility components of Personal Independence Payment. I believe this decision fails to adequately consider my diagnosis, my legal rights under the Equality Act 2010, and the significant impact my condition has on my ability to carry out daily activities safely and consistently.

My Condition

I am autistic. I was formally diagnosed with Autism Spectrum Condition (ASC) by Dr. Patricia Owens, Consultant Psychiatrist, in October 2023. I also have co-occurring diagnoses of Pathological Demand Avoidance (PDA), sensory processing disorder, and anxiety disorder — all directly connected to and exacerbated by my autism. Autism is a lifelong neurological condition recognised under the Equality Act 2010 as a disability.

My Legal Rights — Equality Act 2010

Under the Equality Act 2010 I am a disabled person. The Act places a legal duty on public bodies — including the DWP — to make reasonable adjustments to ensure that disabled people are not placed at a substantial disadvantage. The assessment did not make adequate reasonable adjustments for my autism. I was not given written questions in advance despite requesting this. The assessment did not account for autistic masking or the post-assessment shutdown I experienced for three days following the appointment.

Autistic Masking

Research consistently demonstrates that many autistic people present as more capable in assessment settings than they are in everyday life. This is not deliberate misrepresentation — it is an involuntary and exhausting coping mechanism. The consequences include post-assessment burnout, increased anxiety, and deterioration in mental health — all of which I experienced. The assessor's observations cannot be taken as an accurate representation of my day to day functioning.

Legal Basis

I rely on the Equality Act 2010, the Social Security (PIP) Regulations 2013, the UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities Article 19, and the DWP PIP Assessment Guide which specifically acknowledges fluctuating and hidden conditions. I submit that placing me in the LCWRA group is required under Regulation 35 given the substantial risk that work related activity would pose to my mental health.

If this reconsideration is unsuccessful I intend to appeal to the First-tier Tribunal and will be seeking advice from a disability rights organisation in preparation for doing so.

Yours faithfully,
James Cooper

Address: 14 Heol yr Ysgol, Pontardawe, SA8 4TN

Date: 17 April 2025

To Whom It May Concern,

I am writing formally to challenge the enforcement action currently being taken against me in respect of the above council tax account, and to request the immediate recall of the bailiff warrant issued in this matter. I am also requesting that the council enter into a formal affordable payment arrangement with me as a matter of urgency.

I wish to make clear from the outset that I am not disputing that council tax is owed. I am disputing the manner in which this debt has been handled, the failure to consider my personal circumstances, and the disproportionate escalation to bailiff enforcement without adequate opportunity for me to engage with the council directly.

Grounds for Challenge

Failure to consider vulnerability: Under the National Standards for Enforcement Agents 2014 and the Local Government Finance Act 1992, local authorities have a duty to identify and make allowances for vulnerable individuals. As a single parent in receipt of means-tested benefits I am considered a vulnerable person. I submit that this vulnerability was not adequately considered before escalating to bailiff enforcement.

Failure to offer a payment arrangement: Prior to the instruction of bailiffs I was not offered a formal opportunity to enter into a payment arrangement. The Ministry of Justice's Taking Control of Goods regulations require that debtors be given reasonable opportunity to pay before enforcement action is taken. I submit this process was not followed correctly.

My Legal Rights

Under the Taking Control of Goods Regulations 2013 bailiffs must follow a strict process before entering a property. Under the Human Rights Act 1998 Article 8, enforcement action disproportionately impacting a family with dependent children must be carefully considered. Under the Equality Act 2010 I am entitled to have my circumstances considered fully throughout this process.

What I Am Requesting

I formally request: the immediate recall of the bailiff warrant; the freezing of all additional bailiff fees; a formal affordable payment arrangement of £10 per month reflecting my genuine financial position; and written confirmation that no further enforcement action will be taken while a payment arrangement is maintained.

If the council is unwilling to act I intend to submit a formal complaint, refer this matter to the Local Government and Social Care Ombudsman, and seek further legal advice.

Yours faithfully,
Michelle Davies

BLUE BADGE — FORMAL CHALLENGE OF REFUSAL DECISION

Name: Patricia Evans  |  DOB: 12 May 1955

Reference: BB/2024/339217  |  Date: 17 April 2025

To Whom It May Concern,

I am writing to formally challenge the decision made on 2 April 2025 to refuse my application for a Blue Badge. I believe this decision does not accurately reflect the full extent of my mobility difficulties, fails to consider the supporting medical evidence provided, and is inconsistent with the eligibility criteria set out under the Transport Act 2000 and the Blue Badge Scheme Local Authority Guidance (England) 2019.

My Conditions

I have been diagnosed with severe osteoarthritis in both knees and hips, peripheral neuropathy, and heart failure. These conditions significantly and permanently affect my ability to walk. On my worst days — which occur the majority of the time — I am unable to walk more than a few metres without severe pain, breathlessness, and risk of falling. I use a walking frame at all times when mobile.

Grounds for Challenge

Failure to apply the correct eligibility criteria: Under the Blue Badge Scheme, a person is eligible if they are unable to walk, or have very considerable difficulty walking, due to a permanent and substantial disability. I submit that I clearly meet this criteria. The decision letter does not adequately explain how this standard was applied to my circumstances and appears to have placed excessive weight on my presentation at assessment rather than my typical day to day mobility.

Failure to consider supporting medical evidence: My application included a letter from my GP Dr. Rhian Morgan confirming my diagnoses and their impact on my mobility, a cardiology report confirming heart failure and its effect on my physical capacity, and a physiotherapy assessment confirming severely restricted mobility and use of a walking frame. I submit that this evidence was not given adequate weight in the decision making process.

Failure to consider fluctuating conditions: The Blue Badge Scheme guidance makes clear that where a condition fluctuates, the assessment must consider the person's ability on their worst days and not solely on the basis of a single assessment appointment. My mobility varies significantly and at its worst I am entirely unable to mobilise independently.

Legal Basis

I rely on the Transport Act 2000 and the Blue Badge Scheme Local Authority Guidance 2019, which sets out the eligibility criteria and the assessment process. I also rely on the Equality Act 2010 — I am a disabled person and am entitled to have the full impact of my disability considered fairly and without disadvantage. Under the Public Sector Equality Duty the local authority must have due regard to the need to eliminate discrimination and advance equality of opportunity for disabled people.

What I Am Requesting

I formally request that this decision be reconsidered in full by a reviewing officer who has not previously been involved in my case. I ask that full weight be given to the supporting medical evidence enclosed, my day to day mobility difficulties, and the correct application of the Blue Badge eligibility criteria.

If this challenge is unsuccessful I intend to request a formal review, submit a complaint to the local authority, and seek advice from a disability rights organisation regarding further options available to me.

Yours faithfully,
Patricia Evans

PERSONAL INDEPENDENCE PAYMENT — MANDATORY RECONSIDERATION REQUEST (MOBILITY COMPONENT UPGRADE)

Name: Robert Griffiths  |  DOB: 30 January 1968

NI Number: GH 11 22 33 J  |  Reference: PIP/2024/771543

Date: 17 April 2025

To Whom It May Concern,

I am writing to formally request a mandatory reconsideration of the decision made on 15 March 2025 to award me the standard rate of the Mobility component of Personal Independence Payment. I accept the award of the standard rate of the Daily Living component. However I submit that the evidence clearly supports an award of the enhanced rate of the Mobility component, and that the failure to award this rate is incorrect and should be reviewed.

I wish to be clear about what is at stake. The difference between the standard and enhanced rate of the Mobility component is not merely financial — it is the difference between being able to access the Motability scheme and being unable to do so. Without the enhanced rate I cannot lease an adapted vehicle. Without an adapted vehicle I am effectively housebound. I respectfully ask that this be given full and careful consideration.

Why I Should Receive the Enhanced Rate

Under the PIP Mobility component, the enhanced rate is awarded where a claimant cannot walk further than 20 metres, or where they can walk further than 20 metres but no more than 50 metres, reliably, repeatedly, safely, and in a reasonable time period.

I have been diagnosed with Multiple Sclerosis, chronic fatigue syndrome, and severe anxiety disorder. The combination of these conditions means that on the majority of days I am unable to walk more than 15 to 20 metres without significant pain, fatigue, loss of balance, and risk of falling. I use a walking stick at all times and have fallen on four occasions in the past three months.

Reliability: Even on days when I can walk a short distance, I cannot do so reliably. My MS causes unpredictable relapses and my fatigue means that what I can manage in the morning I am frequently unable to repeat later in the day.

Safety: My balance difficulties and fall history mean that walking any significant distance without support poses a genuine risk to my safety. My neurologist Dr. Priya Sharma has confirmed in writing that I am at significant risk of falls and should not walk unsupported distances.

Repeatedly: I am unable to walk even short distances repeatedly. The effort required to walk 20 metres leaves me requiring significant rest before I am able to mobilise again.

Supporting Evidence

I am enclosing: a letter from my neurologist Dr. Priya Sharma confirming my MS diagnosis, fall risk, and mobility limitations; a letter from my GP confirming all diagnoses and current treatment; an occupational therapy report confirming my mobility difficulties and equipment requirements; and a letter from my counsellor confirming the psychological impact of my conditions and their effect on my daily functioning.

Legal Basis

Under the Social Security (PIP) Regulations 2013 Schedule 1 Part 3, the enhanced rate of the Mobility component must be awarded where a claimant cannot walk more than 20 metres reliably, repeatedly, safely and in a reasonable time. I submit that I clearly satisfy this descriptor on the majority of days. I also rely on the Equality Act 2010 and the DWP's own guidance on fluctuating conditions which requires assessment across the majority of days rather than on the basis of a single appointment.

If this reconsideration is unsuccessful I intend to appeal to the First-tier Tribunal and will be requesting an oral hearing at which I will be supported by a disability rights adviser.

Yours faithfully,
Robert Griffiths

Important Information

How to Choose a Safe Therapist

This is something I feel strongly about and want to be completely transparent with you about: in the UK, the titles "counsellor" and "therapist" are not legally protected. This means that technically, anyone can call themselves a counsellor or therapist — regardless of whether they have any training, qualifications, or professional registration.

This is a serious issue. An untrained person presenting as a therapist can cause significant and lasting harm to vulnerable people who are seeking genuine help. This is not a theoretical risk — it happens.

Here is what to look for when choosing a therapist:

PROFESSIONAL REGISTRATION

Look for BACP, UKCP, or BPS registration. These bodies have ethical codes and complaints processes.

VERIFIED QUALIFICATION

Ask what qualification they hold and where they studied. A genuine counsellor will answer this clearly and confidently.

INSURANCE

Any legitimate practitioner holds professional indemnity insurance. Always ask.

SUPERVISION

Qualified counsellors attend regular clinical supervision. This protects both client and practitioner.

ICO REGISTRATION

Any therapist handling your personal data should be registered with the Information Commissioner's Office under GDPR.

CHECK THE REGISTER

The BACP register is publicly searchable at bacp.co.uk. Any registered member can be verified in seconds.

My own BACP registration, qualifications, insurance, ICO registration, and supervision arrangements are all in place and available on request. I will always answer any question you have about my professional standing clearly and honestly.

Privacy & Confidentiality

Your Information is Safe With Me

Confidentiality — Everything you share with me is treated with complete confidentiality. What you tell me stays between us. I will never share your information with anyone without your explicit consent.

The limits of confidentiality — In line with BACP ethical guidelines, there are rare situations where I may need to break confidentiality without your consent. These are: if I believe there is a serious and immediate risk to your life or someone else's; if I am required to do so by law; or if a court orders disclosure. I will always discuss this with you in our first session so there are no surprises.

GDPR & Data Protection — I am registered with the Information Commissioner's Office (ICO) in compliance with the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR). Any personal information you share with me is stored securely, password protected, and encrypted. Only I have access to your data.

What information I hold — I may hold your name, contact details, session notes, and relevant personal information shared during sessions. This information is held only for as long as necessary and in line with BACP guidance on record retention.

Your rights — Under GDPR you have the right to access the information I hold about you, request corrections, and request deletion of your data. To exercise any of these rights please contact me directly.

Supervision — As a qualified practitioner I attend regular clinical supervision. Your material may be discussed in supervision in an anonymised form as part of my professional development and to ensure you receive the best possible care. Your identity will never be disclosed.
